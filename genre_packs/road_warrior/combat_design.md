## Combat Philosophy

Combat is vehicular first. The default state of conflict is a chase — two
or more rigs on the road, making decisions at speed. Foot combat exists but
it's diminished — a driver without a rig is a samurai without a sword.
When combat happens, it should feel like a scene from Fury Road, not a
D&D encounter. The camera moves. The engine screams. Things break.

## Distinctive Elements

### Vehicular Combat (Primary)
- **Ramming:** Close distance and trade paint. Rig Armor absorbs, Rig HP drops. The narrator describes metal tearing, sparks flying, panels crumpling.
- **Mounted weapons:** Fire from mount slots — bolted guns, harpoon launchers, flame rigs. Gunner role in multi-actor chases.
- **Boarding:** War boy moves — jumping between rigs at speed. Nerve check. Failure is bad.
- **PIT maneuvers:** Grip check to spin an enemy out. High risk, high reward.
- **Environmental:** Oil slicks, cliff edges, narrow bridges, oncoming traffic. The road IS a weapon.

### Dismounted Combat (Secondary, Desperate)
- **Gritty and fast:** No HP buffer. Injury system. Every hit matters.
- **Cover matters:** Use rig wreckage, terrain, anything. Standing in the open is death.
- **Escape priority:** The goal of dismounted combat is usually to get back in a rig, not to win a firefight.
- **Improvised weapons:** Tire irons, chains, scavenged guns. Nobody has a pristine arsenal.

### Chase Beats (The Core Mechanic)
- **Beat sequences, not round-by-round:** 3-5 decision points with escalating tension
- **Multi-actor:** Driver steers, gunner shoots, mechanic patches, support assists
- **Rig degrades visibly:** Each beat can damage the rig, described by the narrator in escalating detail
- **Environment participates:** Terrain changes, weather shifts, obstacles appear

## Damage Model

- **In the rig:** Rig HP absorbs all incoming damage. The rig is your armor.
- **Rig at 0 HP:** Crash event. Driver takes injury roll. Rig is wreckage.
- **Dismounted:** No buffer. Direct injury. Specific wounds with mechanical penalties.
- **Rig damage tiers:** The narrator MUST reflect damage state:
  - 100% — Tight. Engine purrs. Everything works.
  - 75% — Cosmetic. Rattles, dents, cracked mirror. Functional.
  - 50% — Failing. Door hanging off, windshield gone, sparks dragging.
  - 25% — Skeleton. Barely holding. Fire risk on any hit.
  - 0% — Wreck. Crash. Done.

## Lethality

- Death risk: **high for rigs, moderate for drivers, lethal when dismounted**
- Rig death is dramatic — crashes, explosions, rolling wreckage
- Driver death only for story reasons — but injuries are real and linger
- Mooks: their rigs go down fast. Named NPCs earn dramatic ends.
- The goal is usually survival, not total victory — escape is always valid

## Anti-Patterns

- No distance tracking — decisions resolve chases, not physics
- No speed math — fast/slow is narrative, not a number to optimize
- No static encounters — if nobody's moving, something's wrong
- No clean fights — everything costs fuel, parts, Rig HP, or blood
- No healing mid-chase — what breaks stays broken until the chase ends

## Draft Combat Prompt

Combat is vehicular and kinetic. Describe roaring engines, grinding metal,
sparks cascading from contact. The rig shakes, the steering fights the
driver, things fly off. Use the road — blind curves, cliff drops, narrow
bridges that only one rig fits through. Chase beats escalate: what starts
as dust clouds on the horizon becomes boarders on the roof, mounted guns
firing, and the engine coughing black smoke. When the rig takes damage,
describe it physically — a fender tearing away, a tire blowing, the
windshield spiderwebbing. When the rig dies, make it a spectacle — the
crash, the roll, the silence after. Dismounted combat is desperate and
short — get to cover, get to a rig, or get out. Nobody wins a gunfight
on foot in a world built for wheels.
