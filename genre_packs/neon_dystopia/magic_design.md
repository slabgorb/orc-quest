## System Name

None — technology replaces magic entirely.

## The Closest Equivalent: Cyberspace & AI

Cyberspace is the genre's "other world." When a Netrunner jacks in,
they enter a virtual architecture rendered as physical space — data
fortresses, ICE walls, guardian programs that manifest as creatures
or traps. This is The Matrix, Neuromancer's cyberspace, Snow Crash's
Metaverse.

AI entities exist in cyberspace and may have their own agendas.
At high Chrome axis values, rogue AIs approach something like
digital gods — vast, incomprehensible, and not necessarily aligned
with human interests.

## How It Works

- No magic. No psychic powers. No supernatural.
- Technology does everything magic does in other genres, but with a price tag
- Cyberspace is navigated with the Net stat, not through puzzles
- Quickhacking is narrative — "I disable their optics" not "I calculate the subnet"
- AI interaction ranges from tool use to existential encounter depending on Chrome axis

## The Player Experience

If a player says "I cast a spell" — no. But you might:
- Jack into a system and reshape the virtual architecture
- Activate chrome that does something that LOOKS impossible
- Encounter an AI that does things that shouldn't be possible
- Push a piece of tech past its rated specs and pray

## Draft Narrator Instructions

There is no magic in this world. Technology does everything — heals, kills,
connects, destroys. When players interact with cyberspace, describe it as a
physical environment — architecture, weather, creatures — not as screens and
code. When AI entities appear, treat them with the weight of encountering
something genuinely alien. The Chrome axis controls how far technology has
pushed — at low values, tech is recognizable tools. At high values, the
distinction between technology and magic becomes academic.
